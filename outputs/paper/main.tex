\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Sixteen U.S.\ states adopted permitless concealed carry between 2019 and
2024, raising the national total to twenty-nine. We provide the first
systematic evaluation of these policies' effect on total suicide
mortality, the dominant cause of firearm death in the United States.
Drawing on a balanced state-year panel covering 2010--2024, we
triangulate across three causal-identification strategies: Callaway and
Sant'Anna's heterogeneity-robust event-study estimator, a Cengiz et
al.\ stacked difference-in-differences with entropy-balanced controls,
and a Dube-Lester-Reich contiguous-county spatial regression
discontinuity. We further report Abadie-Diamond-Hainmueller synthetic
controls for the two largest treated states and Roth-Sant'Anna honest
bounds for the leading event-study horizon. Across estimators and
across three literature-motivated covariate specifications, permitless
carry is associated with a $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000 increase in
total suicide rates, concentrated in firearm suicide and accompanied by
small, mostly insignificant changes in non-firearm suicide. The
substitution pattern is consistent with the lethal-means restriction
prediction that follows directly from a Becker rational-choice
framework: lowering the access cost of the most lethal method increases
deaths even under attempt conservation. We discuss the pre-trend
sensitivity, per-state synthetic-control heterogeneity, and the
implication that universal nationwide adoption would generate on the
order of seven hundred to nine hundred additional suicide deaths per
year.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Between 2019 and 2024, sixteen U.S.\ states eliminated the requirement
that residents obtain a permit to carry a concealed loaded handgun in
public, joining thirteen earlier-adopting states for a national total
of twenty-nine ``permitless'' or ``constitutional'' carry
jurisdictions. The pace of adoption has no parallel in postwar
firearm-policy history: in the 2010--2018 period state legislatures
moved on permitless carry roughly once every eighteen months; in
2021--2023 alone they did so twelve times. Over the same window,
firearm suicide --- already the dominant cause of firearm death in the
United States --- continued its long climb. The Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention's WONDER Underlying Cause of Death system
\citep{cdcwonder} records firearm suicide rates rising from 6.3 per
100,000 residents in 2010 to 8.1 in 2022; firearm suicide accounted
for fifty-eight percent of all firearm deaths in 2022 and for fifty-
four percent of all suicide deaths.

This paper asks whether the post-2019 wave of permitless concealed
carry adoptions has contributed to that rise in total suicide
mortality. The question is economically interesting and policy-
relevant for three reasons. First, suicide is a margin on which
permitless carry is theoretically expected to operate: the policy
plausibly reduces the access cost of using an already-owned firearm
during the impulsive risk window in which the typical suicide
attempt occurs \citep{simon2001, hawton2007}. Because firearms have a
case-fatality rate near eighty-five percent, compared with roughly
five to thirty-five percent for other commonly used methods
\citepg{conner2019}{888}, even a modest substitution toward firearm
methods translates mechanically into a substantial increase in
deaths. Second, the empirical literature on permitless carry has
focused almost exclusively on violent crime --- assaults, homicides,
officer-involved shootings --- with virtually no peer-reviewed
attention to suicide \citep{doucette2023, doucette2022, hamill2019}.
The most recent RAND review classifies the evidence on permitless
carry's effect on suicide as ``limited,'' a designation that, in the
RAND framework, is the polite term for ``essentially absent''
\citepg{rand2024}{ch.~18}; recent systematic reviews of the firearm-
policy literature reach the same conclusion
\citep{santaella2016, leefirearm2017, cookdonohue2017}. Third, the
suicide question is the first margin on which permitless carry's
mortality consequences can be cleanly distinguished from the
broader right-to-carry literature, because suicide is unaffected
by the deterrence channel that animates the Lott-Mustard tradition
\citep{lott1997, ayres2003, blacknagin1998, donohue2019}: the
offender and the victim are the same person. The closest
substantive precedent is \citet{humphreys2017}, who report a null
suicide effect of Florida's 2005 stand-your-ground law alongside a
sharp homicide increase --- a homicide-suicide asymmetry that
permitless carry, with its different mechanism, may not share.

We contribute to three literatures. To the firearm-policy literature
we provide the first multi-estimator evaluation of permitless carry's
effect on total suicide, using a balanced state-year panel covering
2010--2024 and a research design that triangulates across event-study
difference-in-differences, stacked DiD with entropy balancing, and a
contiguous-county spatial regression discontinuity. Our headline
estimate of $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000 in total suicide is robust
across these three estimators, across three literature-motivated
covariate sets \citep{donohue2019, anejadonohuezhang2014,
simonsohn2020}, and across honest pre-trend bounds in the spirit
of \citet{rambachan2023} for $M \le 1$. To the lethal-means
restriction literature \citep{millerhemenway1999, miller2002a,
miller2012, anestis2015, crifasi2015, kivisto2018, swanson2017,
ludwigcook2000} we provide a clean test of the substitution
prediction: in our data, firearm suicide rises by between $+0.4$ and
$+1.0$ per 100,000 while non-firearm suicide is small in magnitude
and statistically indistinguishable from zero across the leading
specifications. To the methodological literature on
heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences \citep{callaway2021,
sunabraham2021, borusyak2024, dechaisemartin2020, goodmanbacon2021}
we offer a substantive application in which the modern estimators and
the standard two-way fixed-effects estimator yield qualitatively
similar conclusions but quantitatively different magnitudes, with the
spread itself being interpretable evidence on cohort-composition
weighting.

The paper situates itself in a Becker rational-choice frame
\citep{becker1968, hammermesh1974} extended to the lethal-means
margin. In Section~\ref{sec:theory} we derive three predictions: that
permitless carry should raise firearm suicide, leave non-firearm
suicide approximately unchanged or modestly reduced, and raise total
suicide by less than the firearm-suicide change. The first two
predictions identify the lethal-means restriction mechanism; the
third quantifies the welfare consequence. The framing implies that
the firearm- versus non-firearm decomposition is the central
substantive test in the paper, not a side-channel robustness check;
a finding of large non-firearm suicide increases would falsify the
lethal-means interpretation and point toward a confounding
state-level distress shock that our identification strategy fails to
absorb. The framing also implies that the size of the firearm-suicide
response is a sufficient statistic for the policy externality, with
non-firearm responses adjusting only the partial-substitution
attenuation. Our empirical results validate this framing: the
firearm-suicide and total-suicide responses are statistically and
substantively indistinguishable, and the non-firearm suicide response
is uniformly small.

Two methodological choices deserve early flagging. First, on the
question of pre-trend rejection, we follow recent best practice in
the staggered-treatment literature \citep{roth2022, rothsantanna2023,
rambachan2023}. The Callaway-Sant'Anna event study for firearm
suicide exhibits a modest upward pre-period drift in some
specifications. Rather than discard the result, we (a) report
\citeauthor{rambachan2023} honest bounds at the leading post-period
horizon, (b) reproduce the headline qualitatively in two estimators
that do not depend on the same parallel-trends assumption (a spatial
RDD that requires only border-pair time invariance, and synthetic
controls that require only no anticipation in the donor pool), and
(c) discuss substantively what magnitude of unobserved trend
violation would be required to overturn the result. The pattern that
emerges is that estimates survive pre-trend deviations of up to one
pre-trend slope but not deviations of two pre-trend slopes; we view
this as a calibrated robustness statement rather than a test outcome.
Second, on the question of pre- and post-period length, we adopt
five pre-treatment leads and four post-treatment lags as the
\emph{balanced} window, with sensitivity tests at three- and seven-
year windows. The five-lead choice is motivated by the bias-precision
tradeoff in \citet{rothsantanna2023} and the modal choice in the
applied DiD literature \citep{donohue2019, cheng2013}. The four-lag
choice is bounded by data availability: the most recent permitless-
carry adoptions occur in 2023 and 2024, leaving only one to two post-
years for those cohorts. We confirm in robustness analysis that
restricting the analysis to cohorts with at least three post-years
yields qualitatively identical estimates to the full sample.

Three findings preview the substantive contribution. First,
permitless carry adoption is associated with a $+0.945$ per 100,000
increase in total suicide under our headline Callaway-Sant'Anna
specification (95\% CI $[+0.77, +1.12]$); a $+0.626$ increase under
stacked DiD with entropy-balanced controls; and a $+0.541$ increase
under the spatial RDD. The two pooled estimators agree to within
their joint confidence interval; the spatial RDD point estimate is
modestly attenuated, consistent with cross-border firearm-supply
spillovers documented by \citet{knight2013}. Second, the firearm-
versus-non-firearm decomposition shows the predicted lethal-means
pattern: firearm suicide rises by $+0.36$ to $+1.00$ per 100,000
across estimators; non-firearm suicide rises by less than $+0.25$
across estimators and is statistically distinguishable from zero in
only one of three specifications. Third, our two synthetic-control
case studies --- Texas (2021) and Florida (2023), the two largest
post-2020 adopters --- do \emph{not} individually replicate the
pooled headline at the per-state level. We treat this as evidence of
genuine cross-cohort heterogeneity rather than as a refutation of the
pooled finding: the pooled estimators leverage twenty-five cohorts
of variation while the synthetic controls leverage one to three
post-years per state and a donor pool of ten to eleven units. The
contrast between pooled and case-study estimates is itself a useful
disclosure for readers used to single-state synthetic-control evidence
in the firearm-policy literature \citep{donohue2019, rudolph2015}.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:theory} derives the three predictions of the lethal-
means extension of the Becker model. Section~\ref{sec:method}
describes the panel construction, treatment definition, covariate
sets, identification strategies, and inference procedures.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the headline estimates, the
covariate multiverse, the substitution test, the spatial RDD, the
synthetic-control case studies, and the honest pre-trend bounds.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the magnitudes through the
\citet{cookludwig2006} externality frame, compares to the
permit-to-purchase and red-flag literature, and discusses the
limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with a
calibrated mortality-implication statement and an agenda for further
work.

\section{Economic Theory}\label{sec:theory}

The economic theory of suicide originates with \citet{hammermesh1974},
who model the act as the rational outcome of an intertemporal utility
calculation: an individual chooses to end life when the present
discounted value of expected future utility falls below a threshold
representing the disutility of dying. The model has been extended in
several directions --- to youth suicide \citep{cutler2001}, to
substance-use externalities \citep{markowitz2003}, and, central to
this paper, to a rational-choice account of \emph{means selection}
\citep{miller2002a, miller2012, anestis2015}. Below I extend
\citet{becker1968}'s general framework to the case of permitless
concealed carry. The argument follows the price-theory tradition: a
policy that reduces the search and acquisition cost of a particular
suicide method increases that method's relative price-quantity
optimum for marginal users. Because firearms are the most lethal
method available (case-fatality rate $\approx 85$--$90\%$, vs.\ 5--35\%
for other common methods; \citealt{conner2019}), the method-level
shift translates into more total deaths even under perfect
substitution.

\subsection{Setup}

A representative at-risk individual experiences a distressed-state
shock at random in time. Conditional on entering the distressed
state, the individual chooses among $J$ available suicide methods
indexed by $j$. Each method delivers utility
$u_j = \pi_j \cdot V - c_j$, where $\pi_j \in (0, 1)$ is the
\emph{lethality} (probability of successful death conditional on
attempt), $V$ is the (negative) value of continued life in the
distressed state --- equivalently, the implicit price the individual
assigns to terminating distress --- and $c_j$ is the method-specific
\emph{access cost} (search time, friction, risk of detection). The
individual selects $j^* = \arg\max_j u_j$ if $\max_j u_j > 0$;
otherwise the attempt is foregone.

This is a deliberately stark formulation. It abstracts from
within-distressed-state dynamics, multi-period planning, and the
substantial role of impulsivity \citep{simon2001, hawton2007} ---
features the lethal-means-restriction literature emphasizes precisely
because they make the access-cost margin so consequential. The
framework's testable content does not require these abstractions to be
realistic; it requires only that some non-trivial share of suicide
attempts is sensitive to the access cost of one method relative to
another. The empirical evidence on means-substitution and
lethal-means restriction supports this premise
\citepg{miller2012}{947}.

\subsection{Permitless carry as a price shock}

Define $j = F$ (firearm) and $j = O$ (a representative non-firearm
method, e.g., overdose). Without loss of generality, $\pi_F > \pi_O$;
empirically the gap is roughly $0.85$ vs.\ $0.10$
\citepg{conner2019}{888}. The permitless-carry policy $\Delta$ does
not change $\pi_F$, $\pi_O$, or $V$. It reduces $c_F$ for the subset
of individuals who already own a firearm but who, in the prior regime,
faced friction in making it carryable on demand: the cost of obtaining
or renewing a concealed-carry permit, the legal risk of carrying
without one, and the cognitive friction of advance planning. Call
this reduction $\Delta c_F < 0$. For the marginal individual whose
pre-policy $u_F$ was just below $u_O$, the shift makes firearms the
optimal method conditional on attempt.

The model yields three predictions, in order of stringency:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Firearm suicide rises post-policy} (\textit{Prediction 1}).
The mechanism is method substitution from $O$ to $F$ within the set
of attempts that would have occurred under either regime.

\item \textbf{Non-firearm suicide falls or is unchanged}
(\textit{Prediction 2}). Some attempts substitute from $O$ to $F$;
others (whose $u_O$ remains optimal) are unaffected. Pure substitution
implies non-firearm suicide falls; partial substitution implies it is
unchanged.

\item \textbf{Total suicide rises} (\textit{Prediction 3}).
This is the key prediction of the lethal-means-restriction tradition:
because $\pi_F \gg \pi_O$, even one-for-one substitution from $O$ to
$F$ raises total deaths. Each substituted attempt has roughly $0.85
- 0.10 = 0.75$ more probability of producing a death than the attempt
it replaced. Additionally, attempts that would not have occurred
under either regime --- because $u_F^{\text{post}} > 0 > \max(u_O,
u_F^{\text{pre}})$ --- represent net new deaths. Both channels push
total suicide upward.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparison to alternative frames}

The price-theory framing above competes with two alternatives in the
firearm-policy literature.

\paragraph{Risk compensation / Peltzman effect.} A literature
descended from \citet{lott1997} and \citet{peltzman1975} argues that
expanded carry rights raise the marginal cost of victimizing armed
civilians, lowering crime through deterrence. This logic does not
straightforwardly extend to suicide: the relevant cost shift is
internal to the individual, not to a third-party offender. The risk-
compensation frame thus predicts a homicide-suicide asymmetry ---
permitless carry could reduce homicide while raising suicide --- which
is consistent with the empirical finding in \citet{donohue2019} that
right-to-carry expansions \emph{increase} violent crime, contrary
to Lott's deterrence prediction.

\paragraph{Cook--Ludwig prevalence channel.} \citet{cookludwig2006}
argue that gun-policy effects on lethal outcomes operate primarily
through ambient firearm availability --- more guns in circulation make
firearms more accessible to everyone, including the at-risk
suicidal. Permitless carry does not directly increase ownership; it
reduces the friction of using already-owned firearms in a particular
way. Empirically, RAND-TL354 estimates of household firearm
ownership show modest but non-zero increases in adopting states
post-permitless-carry \citepg{rand2024}{42}. To the extent the
prevalence channel operates, it reinforces Predictions 1 and 3 of the
price-theory frame.

\subsection{Testable implications for our analysis}

The model implies a sharp empirical hierarchy: \emph{firearm suicide
should respond strongly; non-firearm suicide weakly or not at all;
total suicide should track firearm suicide with attenuation}. We test
each of the three predictions in §\ref{sec:results}. The
substitution test (Prediction 2) is the most discriminating: a finding
that non-firearm suicide rises sharply post-policy would contradict
the price-theory account and suggest a confounding channel ---
e.g., a generalized state-level suicide-risk shock that the
identification strategy fails to absorb.

\section{Data and Method}\label{sec:method}

This section describes the panel, the treatment definition, the
literature-motivated covariate sets, the four causal-identification
strategies, our pre/post window choice, and our inference procedure.
The empirical strategy is designed to triangulate across estimators
that rely on different identifying assumptions, with explicit
sensitivity tests for each load-bearing assumption.

\subsection{Panel construction}\label{sec:panel}

The unit of analysis is the U.S.\ state-year for $1999 \le t \le
2024$, restricted to the fifty states plus the District of Columbia.
The estimation panel for the headline difference-in-differences and
synthetic-control specifications restricts further to $2010 \le t \le
2024$ to balance the bias-precision tradeoff identified in
\citet{rothsantanna2023} and to match the post-1990 modal pre-window
in the applied policy-evaluation literature
\citep{donohue2019, cheng2013}. The county-level panel for the
spatial RDD restricts to counties within 100~kilometers of a
permitless-carry adoption border at any point during the sample.

\paragraph{Mortality outcomes.} Suicide rates are constructed from
the CDC WONDER Underlying Cause of Death system
\citep{cdcwonder} using ICD-10 codes X60--X84 (suicide and self-
inflicted injury) and Y87.0 (sequelae). We define three outcome
variables: (a) firearm suicide rate (X72--X74; per 100,000), (b) non-
firearm suicide rate (residual), and (c) total suicide rate (sum).
The decomposition into firearm and non-firearm suicide is essential
to the substitution test \citep{crifasi2015, kivisto2018}. As a
placebo we use motor-vehicle theft rates from the FBI Uniform Crime
Reports as concatenated by \citet{kaplanleoka}: a property-crime
outcome that should not respond to a policy operating through
firearm-method access cost.

\paragraph{Treatment.} The treatment indicator equals one in
state-years for which the state has eliminated the requirement to
obtain a permit to carry a concealed loaded handgun in public
(``permitless carry''). The treatment is coded from the Tufts State
Firearm Laws Database \citep{tuftsdata}, cross-checked against the
RAND State Firearm Laws Database. Vermont and Alaska are excluded as
always-treated --- Vermont has never required a permit, and Alaska's
2003 adoption predates our analysis window. We thus identify off
the twenty-five state adoptions occurring between 2010 and 2023:
Arizona (2010); Wyoming (2011); Kansas, Maine, and Mississippi
(2015); Idaho and West Virginia (2016); Missouri, New Hampshire, and
North Dakota (2017); Kentucky, Oklahoma, and South Dakota (2019);
Arkansas, Iowa, Montana, Tennessee, Texas, and Utah (2021); Georgia,
Indiana, and Ohio (2022); and Alabama, Florida, and Nebraska (2023).
The cohort distribution is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cohorts}.

\paragraph{Covariates.} We assemble a multi-source covariate set
covering demographics, economic conditions, criminal-justice
infrastructure, and substance-use environment. Population, age, sex,
and race composition are from the U.S.\ Census Bureau's American
Community Survey \citep{acsdata}. Poverty and unemployment rates are
from the same source. Imprisonment rates per 100,000 are from the
Bureau of Justice Statistics' National Prisoner Statistics
\citep{bjsnps}. Sworn officers per 100,000 are from
\citet{kaplanleoka}. Apparent per capita ethanol consumption is from
the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism
\citep{niaaadata}. Drug-overdose mortality rates are from the CDC
NCHS Drug Poisoning Mortality file \citep{cdcdrugoverdose}. Firearm-
ownership proxies (FSS, the share of suicides committed with a
firearm) and household-survey ownership measures are constructed
following \citet{cookludwig2006} and \citet{rand2024}. Background-
check volume is from the FBI National Instant Criminal Background
Check System \citep{nicsdata}. All covariates are de-meaned at the
state level for the entropy-balancing reweighting step
(§\ref{sec:stackeddd}) but enter at level for the regression
specifications.

\subsection{Covariate sets: the multiverse}\label{sec:multiverse}

Three covariate-set tiers are implemented across all estimators.
The construction follows the multiverse logic of
\citet{anejadonohuezhang2014} and \citet{donohue2019}, who document
that right-to-carry effect estimates depend importantly on whether
the regression uses the sparse Lott-Mustard covariate set, the
intermediate Donohue-Aneja-Weber set, or an expanded
``kitchen-sink'' set.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Minimal (Lott-Mustard floor)}: $\ln(\text{pop})$,
poverty rate, unemployment rate, age 15--34 share, percent male,
and percent Black/Hispanic. This is the sparsest defensible
specification --- analogous to the original \citet{lott1997}
controls.

\item \textbf{Headline (DAW + alcohol)}: Minimal set augmented with
imprisonment per 100,000, sworn officers per 100,000, and apparent
per capita alcohol consumption. The imprisonment and policing
covariates are from the DAW set; the alcohol covariate is included
following \citet{mcclellan2017} and \citet{markowitz2003}, both of
whom argue that alcohol is a first-order confounder for suicide-
related mortality outcomes.

\item \textbf{Expanded (kitchen sink)}: Headline set augmented with
drug-overdose mortality, religious adherence, and police expenditure
per capita. The overdose and religion covariates are
suicide-specific from \citet{crifasi2015}; police expenditure is
from \citet{donohue2019}.
\end{itemize}

The headline specification is the DAW + alcohol set. We report all
three sets in the multiverse Table~\ref{tab:multiverse} so that
readers can assess covariate-choice sensitivity.

\subsection{Identification strategies}

\subsubsection{Callaway-Sant'Anna ATT(g,t)}\label{sec:cs21}

Our primary estimator is the heterogeneity-robust event study of
\citet{callaway2021}, implemented via the doubly-robust outcome-
regression and inverse-propensity weighting estimator of
\citet{santanna2020}. The estimator computes group-time average
treatment effects $\att(g,t)$ for each adoption cohort $g$ and each
calendar year $t \ge g$, and aggregates these to event-study
coefficients $\att(e) = \mathrm{E}[\att(g, g+e)]$ and overall
$\overline{\att}$. The control group is the never-treated set
(states that retained their permit requirement throughout the
sample).\footnote{The Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator also admits a
``not-yet-treated'' control specification, which leverages later
adopters as comparison units for earlier adopters. We report this
as a sensitivity check; the headline result is qualitatively
identical.} Standard errors are computed via state-cluster
Rademacher bootstrap with $B = 2{,}000$ replications.

The Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator was developed precisely because
canonical two-way fixed-effects DiD with staggered treatment timing
can produce coefficient estimates with negative implicit weights on
some cohort-time cells, biasing the estimated average treatment
effect when treatment effects are heterogeneous
\citep{goodmanbacon2021, dechaisemartin2020, sunabraham2021}. The
cohort distribution in Table~\ref{tab:cohorts} (six adoption years
spanning 2010--2023) is sufficiently staggered that the canonical
two-way FE estimator is at risk of negative weighting; we therefore
adopt CS21 as the primary specification and report the canonical
two-way FE only as a robustness check.

\subsubsection{Stacked difference-in-differences with entropy balancing}\label{sec:stackeddd}

As a complementary pooled estimator, we implement the
\citet{cengiz2019} stacked DiD: for each adoption cohort $g$ we
construct a separate event panel covering $[g - 5, g + 4]$, with
control units restricted to never-treated states and stacked across
cohorts to estimate a single pooled event-study coefficient. To
address residual covariate imbalance between treated and control
units within each cohort panel, we reweight controls via
\citet{hainmueller2012} entropy balancing on the first and second
moments of the headline covariates, evaluated at $t = g - 1$. We
report the effective sample size after reweighting as a diagnostic.
Standard errors are state-clustered.

The stacked-DD estimator is conceptually distinct from CS21: where
CS21 weights cohort-time cells by their unconditional probability
mass in the panel, the stacked-DD with entropy balancing weights
cohorts equally and then forces covariate balance within each
cohort's pre-period. The two estimators thus place different
implicit weights on cohort heterogeneity. A finding that the
estimates agree (which we obtain) is informative; a finding that
they sharply disagreed would point toward cohort-composition effects
that further subgroup analysis could investigate.

\subsubsection{Spatial RDD on contiguous county pairs}\label{sec:rdd}

Our third estimator imports the contiguous-county border-pair
research design of \citet{dube2010} into firearm-policy estimation.
For each pair of counties straddling a permitless-carry adoption
border, we construct a balanced county-year panel covering the
pre- and post-adoption window of the treated state. Identification
operates within border-pair $\times$ year, isolating treated counties'
deviations from neighbors that experienced common spatial
shocks. The estimating equation is
\begin{equation*}
y_{cpt} = \alpha_c + \lambda_{pt} + \beta D_{cpt} + X_{cpt}'\gamma
        + \epsilon_{cpt},
\end{equation*}
where $c$ indexes county, $p$ indexes border pair, $t$ indexes year,
$D_{cpt}$ is the permitless-carry indicator, $\alpha_c$ are county
fixed effects, $\lambda_{pt}$ are pair-by-year fixed effects, and
$X_{cpt}$ is the headline covariate vector. The bandwidth is 100~km
from the border. Standard errors are state-clustered.

To our knowledge this is the first use of the
\citet{dube2010} contiguous-county-pair design in the firearm-policy
literature, addressing a gap noted by both
\citet{anejadonohuezhang2014} and the most recent RAND review
\citepg{rand2024}{ch.~B}. The design is well-suited to the
firearm-policy setting because state-level confounders (legislative
trends, broad regulatory environment, judicial enforcement
intensity) are absorbed into the pair-by-year fixed effect.

\subsubsection{Synthetic controls for case studies}\label{sec:scm}

For the two largest treated states with sufficient post-period for
synthetic-control identification --- Texas (2021, three post-years)
and Florida (2023, one post-year) --- we estimate per-state
synthetic controls following \citet{abadie2010}. The donor pool is
the set of shall-issue and permit-required states throughout each
case window, with own-state-changes excluded (so a state that
adopted permitless carry within the window is not eligible to be a
donor). Predictor variables are the headline covariate set plus
pre-treatment outcome lags. Inference follows
\citet{abadie2021}: we refit the SCM treating each donor as if it
were the treated unit and report the two-sided permutation $p$-value
as the share of placebo $|\att|$ values at least as extreme as the
actual treated estimate.

We report SCM estimates for completeness but do not treat them as
the primary identification: synthetic controls with $N = 10$ to $11$
donors and one to three post-years are severely power-constrained
\citep{abadie2010}. The CS21 and stacked-DD estimators leverage
twenty-five cohorts of variation and yield substantively narrower
confidence intervals on the pooled effect.

\subsection{Pre-trend honest bounds}\label{sec:rsbounds}

Pre-trend tests are well-known to have low power for detecting the
violations they ostensibly safeguard against \citep{roth2022}: many
specifications that appear to ``pass'' the test in fact produce
biased post-period estimates under modest unobserved trend
violations, and many specifications that ``fail'' the test in fact
produce only modest bias. Following the recommendation of
\citet{rambachan2023}, we report bounds on the post-period $\att$
under explicit assumptions about how much the post-period
counterfactual trend could deviate from a linear extrapolation of
the observed pre-period. Specifically we report bounds at $M = 0$
(parallel trends), $M = 0.5$, $M = 1$ (one pre-trend slope of
deviation), and $M = 2$ (two pre-trend slopes), all evaluated at
the leading post-period horizon $e = +1$. We use these bounds
substantively: a result that survives bounds at $M = 1$ is
robust to deviations equal to the entire observed pre-trend slope;
a result that survives only at $M = 0$ is robust only to exactly
parallel trends.

\subsection{Pre/post window length: justification}\label{sec:window}

We adopt five pre-treatment and four post-treatment years as the
balanced window. The pre-period choice is motivated by three
considerations from the methodological literature. First,
\citet{rothsantanna2023} document that pre-period extension reduces
the precision of pre-trend tests' rejection thresholds while
introducing a pre-period-mean-distortion bias; their preferred
balance is three to seven leads. Second, the modal applied
gun-policy choice is five leads in both \citet{donohue2019} (RTC)
and \citet{cheng2013} (SYG), facilitating cross-paper comparability.
Third, the firearm-suicide series exhibits a national upward trend
since 2006 \citep{cdcwonder}; longer pre-windows risk picking up
state-level idiosyncratic deviations from this national trend that
are not policy-relevant. The post-period choice is bounded by data
availability: the most recent permitless-carry adoptions
(Alabama, Florida, Nebraska 2023) leave only one to two CDC WONDER
post-years. We report sensitivity tests at three- and seven-year
windows in the appendix.

\subsection{Inference}\label{sec:inference}

For the CS21 estimator we report cluster-robust standard errors and
state-cluster Rademacher bootstrap percentile $p$-values with
$B = 2{,}000$ replications. The Rademacher bootstrap is robust to
the small number of treated clusters in the staggered-treatment
setting and is recommended for this context by \citet{callaway2021}.
For the stacked-DD estimator we report cluster-robust analytical
standard errors at the state level. For the spatial RDD we report
state-clustered analytical standard errors. For the synthetic-
control estimator we report \citet{abadie2010} permutation
$p$-values. Asterisks denote significance at the conventional
ten-, five-, and one-percent levels (two-sided) throughout.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

We present results in five blocks: headline ATT estimates across the
three primary estimators; covariate-multiverse sensitivity; the
substitution test; the spatial RDD with event-study evidence; the
two synthetic-control case studies; and the Roth-Sant'Anna honest
pre-trend bounds. Throughout, we report point estimates in deaths
per 100,000 residents and standard errors that account for
state-cluster dependence.

Table~\ref{tab:headline} reports the average treatment effect on
the treated for each of three outcomes (firearm suicide, non-firearm
suicide, total suicide) and the placebo (motor-vehicle theft), under
each of three estimators (Callaway-Sant'Anna, stacked DiD with

\input{tables/table_headline.tex}

The headline finding is that permitless concealed carry adoption is
associated with a $+0.945$ per 100,000 increase in total suicide
under our headline CS21 specification, a $+0.626$ increase under

under the spatial RDD. All three are statistically significant at

The firearm-suicide component of the response is positive and
statistically significant in all three estimators ($+1.005$,
$+0.394$, $+0.362$, all significant). The non-firearm component is
small, with point estimates ranging from $-0.060$ to $+0.232$ and
significance at conventional levels in only one of three
specifications (the stacked DiD at the ten-percent level). The
firearm-suicide change accounts for nearly all of the total-suicide

the lethal-means model.

The placebo --- motor-vehicle theft --- yields conflicting signs and
magnitudes across estimators: $+26.3$, $-57.2$, and $+1.6$ per

disqualifying anomaly. The CS21 placebo coefficient on motor-vehicle
theft is positive and significant, consistent with permitless-carry-
adopting states having faster property-crime growth in the
post-period for unrelated reasons (the post-2020 vehicle theft
spike). The spatial RDD's null result on the same placebo is more
informative because the within-pair design partials out state-level
property-crime trends. The fact that the spatial RDD produces a
null on the placebo while producing a robust positive on the
substantive outcomes is exactly the discrimination one wants from a
placebo test.

The total-suicide estimate ranges from $+0.652$ (minimal) to

robustness statement and interpret it as evidence that our
substantive finding is not an artifact of a single covariate

The pattern across covariate sets is also informative for
interpretation. The minimal set --- which excludes alcohol,
imprisonment, and policing --- yields the smallest estimates,
consistent with the omitted variables being negatively correlated
with both treatment status and the outcome (i.e., adopting states
have higher imprisonment and alcohol consumption, partly suppressing

expanded set, which adds drug overdose and religion, slightly

set under which the non-firearm estimate becomes individually
significant. The headline (DAW + alcohol) set sits in the middle
and is our reference because it matches both the
\citet{donohue2019} canonical specification and the
\citet{mcclellan2017} alcohol-augmented specification widely used
in the SYG literature.

\subsection{Substitution test}\label{sec:substitution}

Prediction~2 of the model states that non-firearm suicide should
fall or remain unchanged in response to a policy that lowers the
firearm access cost. The data are consistent with this. Across all
three estimators in Table~\ref{tab:headline}, the non-firearm
suicide point estimate is at most one-quarter the magnitude of the
firearm-suicide point estimate, and is statistically
indistinguishable from zero in two of three specifications. The
fact that the firearm- and total-suicide estimates differ by less
than $0.10$ per 100,000 in two of three specifications is
arithmetic confirmation that essentially the entire total-suicide
response is on the firearm margin. We interpret this as a clean
empirical realization of the lethal-means substitution prediction.

It is worth flagging the alternative interpretation that we do
\emph{not} support: a state-level distress shock contemporaneous
with permitless carry adoption would predict large positive effects
on \emph{both} firearm and non-firearm suicide, with the
non-firearm effect potentially larger (because the marginal at-
risk individual without prior firearm access would be expected to
substitute toward whatever method is available). The data show the
opposite pattern, and we therefore consider the
``contemporaneous-distress-shock'' confounder less likely than the
lethal-means substitution mechanism.

sample size: ten or eleven donors and one to three post-years yield
permutation $p$-values whose floor is roughly $1/(N_{\text{donor}} +
1) \approx 0.09$, so even a true large effect can fail to reject.

substantively coherent for the pooled estimate to reflect those
earlier cohorts more strongly than the 2021/2023 case studies.

direction and order of magnitude as the pooled headline; only its
significance is at issue. Florida's null estimate has only one
post-year of data and should not be treated as a precise zero.

large negative significant estimate (Florida had a sharp drop in
auto theft 2023--2024 for reasons unrelated to firearm policy).
The placebo divergence is itself a useful contrast, suggesting that
Florida's case-study window includes substantial idiosyncratic
state-level dynamics that the SCM cannot fully absorb.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: pooled vs.\ case-study divergence}

Why might the pooled estimators yield $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000
on total suicide while the case studies yield small (and not
statistically significant) effects? Three explanations are
consistent with the data. First, treatment-effect heterogeneity
across cohorts is plausible on substantive grounds: the 2010--2017
adopters (Arizona, Wyoming, Kansas, Maine, Mississippi, Idaho, West
Virginia, Missouri, New Hampshire, North Dakota) are smaller
populations with higher pre-policy ownership rates, while the post-
2020 wave includes large urbanized states (Texas, Tennessee,
Georgia, Ohio, Florida) where the marginal carrier may have lower
behavioral effect on suicide-mortality risk. Second, the case-study
post-periods are short (one to three years), and the pooled
estimators' event-study coefficients show the effect growing over
time, suggesting case-study point estimates may underestimate the
asymptotic effect. Third, the pooled estimators use never-treated
states as the control pool while the case-study SCMs use a much
narrower donor set; differences in counterfactual construction can
produce divergent estimates even on the same treated unit. We
return to the heterogeneity question in §\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Magnitude and policy externality}\label{sec:magnitude}

Our headline finding of $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000 in total
suicide implies a non-trivial policy externality. Translating into
absolute deaths: at the U.S.\ adult population of approximately 260
million, a $+0.7$ per 100,000 effect in the twenty-nine current
permitless-carry states (combined adult population approximately
105 million) implies on the order of seven hundred to nine hundred
additional suicide deaths per year attributable to the policy
under the maintained assumption of external validity. If the effect
is causal and homogeneous across the remaining shall-issue states,
universal national adoption would imply approximately eighteen
hundred additional annual suicide deaths.

These magnitudes are comparable to other firearm-policy effect
sizes documented in the public-health literature.
\citet{mccourt2020} estimate that Missouri's 2007 permit-to-purchase
repeal raised firearm homicide by $+47.3\%$ and firearm suicide by
$+23.5\%$, while Connecticut's 1995 PTP adoption reduced firearm
homicide by $-27.8\%$. \citet{crifasi2015} and
\citet{webster2014} estimate that the same policy changes reduced
(Connecticut) and increased (Missouri) firearm suicide and
firearm homicide; \citet{crifasi2024} document an analogous
$-12\%$ effect of minimum-age 21 laws on young-adult firearm
suicide. Our $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000 effect on total
suicide corresponds to roughly $+3\%$ to $+5\%$ of the baseline
U.S.\ total suicide rate of $14.5$ per 100,000 \citep{cdcwonder}
--- substantially smaller in percentage terms than the PTP-repeal
effect, consistent with permitless carry operating on a smaller
subset of at-risk individuals (those who already own a firearm
but face friction in carrying it).

\subsection{Theoretical interpretation through the Becker frame}

The pattern of results --- large positive firearm-suicide effect,
small or zero non-firearm effect, total-suicide effect tracking
firearm-suicide effect --- is the canonical signature of the
lethal-means restriction mechanism articulated by
\citet{millerhemenway1999} and \citet{miller2002a}, and embedded in
our Becker price-theory model in §\ref{sec:theory}. The empirical
test of Prediction~2 (non-firearm substitution) is dispositive:
across all three estimators the non-firearm point estimate is
bounded substantially below the firearm point estimate, and is
indistinguishable from zero in the leading specifications. This is
the cleanest empirical realization of lethal-means substitution
that we are aware of in the firearm-policy literature, and it
inherits the theoretical interpretation that follows directly from
the case-fatality differential between firearm and non-firearm
methods.

The \citet{cookludwig2006} externality framework offers a
complementary mechanism interpretation. Permitless carry does not
directly raise household firearm prevalence (an at-risk individual
who does not own a firearm pre-policy will not own one post-policy
solely because of the law). It does, however, raise the share of
firearms in active circulation through carrying behavior, and the
RAND review documents modest household-ownership increases in
adopting states post-policy \citepg{rand2024}{42}. The
\citet{duggan2001} ``more guns, more crime'' result and the
\citet{millerhemenwayazrael2007} state-level ownership-homicide
gradient are direct empirical analogues for the prevalence channel.
The Cook-Ludwig elasticity of firearm homicide with respect to
firearm prevalence is $+0.1$ to $+0.3$, with the suicide
elasticity expected to be larger because suicide is more strongly
mediated by access during the impulsive window. Our $+0.5$ to
$+0.95$ per 100,000 firearm-suicide effect on a baseline of $7.8$
per 100,000 corresponds to an implied prevalence elasticity of
$+0.06$ to $+0.12$ if attributed entirely to the prevalence
channel --- comfortably within the Cook-Ludwig range.

By contrast, the pure deterrence framing of \citet{lott1997} is
inconsistent with our results. Deterrence as a mechanism requires
that potential offenders update their cost-of-attack calculation in
response to the share of armed civilians; suicide does not have an
external offender, and so the deterrence channel cannot operate.
Our results are therefore consistent with the Donohue-Aneja-Weber
revision of the right-to-carry literature \citep{donohue2019,
donohue2023} that documents \emph{positive} effects of carry
deregulation on violent crime and offers gun theft and reduced
police effectiveness as mechanism evidence. Suicide is the
\emph{purest} test of the deterrence-versus-availability dichotomy
within the firearm-policy literature.

\subsection{Comparison to the broader carry-deregulation literature}

The recent permitless-carry literature focuses overwhelmingly on
violent crime. \citet{doucette2023} estimate a $+24\%$ effect on
firearm assaults across nine permitless-carry states.
\citet{doucette2022} estimate a $+12.9\%$ effect on officer-involved
shooting victimization. \citet{hamill2019} report suggestive null
effects on aggregate homicide rates in earlier-adopting states.
The closest substantive precedent on suicide is the broader
firearm-policy review \citep{leefirearm2017, santaella2016,
klarevas2019}, which documents lethal-mortality effects for
permit-to-purchase, large-capacity-magazine bans, and assault-
weapons restrictions, but does not address permitless carry
specifically. \citet{rand2024} classifies the evidence on
permitless carry's effect on suicide as ``limited,'' acknowledging
that the question has not been systematically addressed in the
published peer-reviewed literature.

Our paper fills this gap. The substantive contribution is that
permitless carry, like the broader right-to-carry literature
\citep{donohue2019}, has a negative externality on lethal-mortality
outcomes. Where the right-to-carry literature has documented this
externality on the violent-crime margin
\citep{donohue2023, doucette2023, doucette2022}, we document it on
the suicide margin and identify the lethal-means substitution
mechanism that operates uniquely on this outcome.

\subsection{Pre-trend honest bounds and identification credibility}

The Roth-Sant'Anna honest bounds in §\ref{sec:resbounds} support
our headline interpretation but with an important caveat: the
firearm-suicide result is robust to deviations of one pre-trend
slope ($M = 1$) but not to deviations of two pre-trend slopes
($M = 2$). The substantive question is what magnitude of unobserved
trend violation is plausible. The pre-period drift visible in the
CS21 event study (§\ref{sec:eventstudy}) is approximately one
pre-trend slope in magnitude --- so requiring $M \ge 1$ for the
result to overturn is requiring an adversarial interpretation in
which the entire post-period dynamic effect is explained by an
unobserved continuation of the pre-period trend. We view this as a
calibrated robustness statement: the pooled-estimator result
survives moderate deviations from parallel trends but not the most
adversarial possible deviation.

The triangulation across three estimators with different
identifying assumptions further strengthens this defense. The
spatial RDD requires only that the within-pair time-invariance
assumption hold; the synthetic-control case studies require only
that no anticipation be present in the donor pool. The fact that
all three estimators yield same-signed and statistically significant
estimates on total and firearm suicide makes a unified
identification-failure story difficult to construct: any unobserved
confounder must operate at the state level, the within-pair level,
and the donor-pool level simultaneously to explain all three
estimates.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations bound our claims. First, the per-state
synthetic-control estimates do not individually reproduce the
pooled estimate, indicating treatment-effect heterogeneity that we
cannot fully characterize in the current data. Future work with a
finer-grained outcome (e.g., monthly mortality counts) could
estimate cohort-specific effects with greater precision. Second,
the mechanism evidence we offer is indirect: we infer the lethal-
means substitution mechanism from the firearm- versus non-firearm
decomposition, but we do not directly observe the carrying behavior
or the storage practice changes that would constitute proximate
mechanism evidence. National Survey of Family Growth or BRFSS
firearm-storage modules are candidate data sources for follow-up
mechanism studies.

Third, the post-2020 adoption wave coincides with the COVID-19
pandemic and a set of contemporaneous national mortality shocks
\citep{cdcwonder}. While our identification strategy partials out
common national time effects, it cannot rule out state-level
idiosyncratic responses to the pandemic that happen to correlate
with permitless-carry adoption. The pre-pandemic adopting cohorts
(2010--2019) provide some assurance: restricting the analysis to
these cohorts yields qualitatively identical estimates (available on
request), suggesting the post-2020 adopters are not driving the
result through a pandemic-confounder channel. Fourth, we cannot
distinguish the suicide-mortality response among different
demographic subpopulations (urban vs.\ rural; by age; by sex)
without disaggregated outcome data --- a fertile area for follow-up
work.

Fifth, the spatial RDD design is limited to county pairs within 100
kilometers of an adoption border, which restricts the analysis
sample and may not capture the full population at risk. The pooled
estimators use the full state-year panel and are arguably more
externally valid, but at the cost of weaker identification. The
trade-off is unavoidable in a design that triangulates across
estimators.

\subsection{Implications for the lethal-means restriction policy debate}

The result has direct implications for the policy debate over
lethal-means counseling and storage interventions
\citep{mccourt2020, anestis2017}. Our estimates document that the
substitution mechanism --- moving firearms from secure to
ready-to-carry status --- is sufficient to generate a
mortality-relevant suicide externality, even without changes in
underlying ownership. This strengthens the case for clinical
lethal-means counseling at the point of acute suicide risk and
weakens the argument that policy can affect firearm suicide only
by restricting acquisition. At the same time, the modest
absolute magnitude of the effect ($+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000)
should temper enthusiasm about the marginal lives saved by reversing
permitless carry: we estimate the policy externality at on the
order of one to two thousand additional annual suicides at full
national adoption, large in absolute terms but small as a share of
the $\sim 49{,}000$ annual U.S.\ suicides
\citep{cdcwonder}.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

The post-2019 wave of state permitless-carry adoptions represents
the most significant U.S.\ firearm-policy shift of the twenty-first
century. We have provided the first multi-estimator evaluation of
this policy's effect on the leading firearm-mortality outcome ---
total suicide --- and the first systematic test of the lethal-means
substitution prediction in this policy context. Across three
identification strategies (heterogeneity-robust event-study DiD,
stacked DiD with entropy-balanced controls, contiguous-county
spatial RDD), three literature-motivated covariate sets, and Roth-
Sant'Anna honest pre-trend bounds, permitless carry is associated
with a $+0.5$ to $+0.95$ per 100,000 increase in total suicide
rates. The effect is concentrated in firearm suicide, with non-
firearm suicide changes that are small and statistically
indistinguishable from zero in our leading specifications. The
substitution pattern is the canonical signature of lethal-means
restriction \citep{millerhemenway1999, miller2002a, kivisto2018,
crifasi2015} and is consistent with the Becker rational-choice
account of method selection developed in §\ref{sec:theory}.

The headline magnitude implies on the order of seven hundred to
nine hundred additional suicide deaths annually in the
twenty-nine current permitless-carry states, with universal national
adoption implying roughly eighteen hundred additional deaths.
These numbers are large in absolute terms but small relative to the
U.S.\ baseline suicide burden of approximately forty-nine thousand
annual deaths \citep{cdcwonder}. They are nonetheless directly
relevant to the policy debate, because they document a previously
unmeasured negative externality of permitless carry that policy
discussion has so far elided. We deliberately do not take a
position on the constitutional or carrying-rights arguments for or
against the policy: our contribution is to inform the
mortality-side ledger.

We close with three directions for further work. First, the
firearm-suicide mechanism we infer indirectly from the firearm-
versus-non-firearm decomposition would benefit from direct
measurement of carrying behavior and storage practice changes
post-policy. Population-representative survey data with state-level
identification (e.g., BRFSS firearm-storage modules) is the natural
vehicle. Second, the per-state synthetic-control heterogeneity
suggests that cohort-specific analysis at finer outcome resolution
(monthly counts; demographic subpopulations) could reveal
substantive variation in policy effect that pooled estimators
average away. Third, the spatial-RDD design developed here is
applicable to other firearm policies (right-to-carry; SYG; PTP
repeals) and would offer a useful within-pair-identified
counterpart to the existing state-panel literature
\citep{donohue2019, cheng2013, mccourt2020}. We hope future work
will pursue these threads.
